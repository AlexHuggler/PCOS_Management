PCOS and Insulin Resistance: The Hidden Connection That Drives Symptoms

Insulin sits upstream of so much of what people with PCOS notice every day — irregular cycles, acne, hair changes, weight that will not budge — and it does so even at a healthy weight.

By the CycleBalance Team · Reviewed against peer-reviewed research

Key takeaways

  • Roughly 50–70% of people with PCOS have insulin resistance, and so do many in the lean PCOS group — body weight is not a reliable filter.
  • The cascade is mechanistic: high insulin pushes the ovaries to make more androgens and lowers SHBG, which raises free testosterone.
  • HbA1c alone can miss it. Fasting insulin, fasting glucose with a HOMA-IR calculation, and an oral glucose tolerance test with insulin are far more sensitive.
  • Lifestyle is genuinely first-line per the 2023 international PCOS guideline; metformin and inositol have specific, evidence-backed roles when more support is needed.

1. The 50–70% number — and why lean PCOS is part of it

If you take only one number from this article, take this one. Across decades of research, somewhere between 50% and 70% of women with PCOS have measurable insulin resistance — and the figure is high regardless of body size.

The original work goes back to Dunaif and colleagues in 1989, who used insulin clamps (the gold-standard method) to demonstrate that women with PCOS had reduced insulin sensitivity that could not be explained by obesity alone[1]. That intrinsic, partly weight-independent insulin resistance was confirmed in detail by Stepto and colleagues in Human Reproduction, who used euglycaemic-hyperinsulinaemic clamps in lean and overweight women with PCOS and found that insulin resistance was present in 75% of lean and 62% of overweight participants with PCOS — meaning, if anything, lean women were not protected[2]. The seminal review by Diamanti-Kandarakis and Dunaif then synthesized hundreds of studies to argue that insulin resistance is a core, intrinsic feature of PCOS, not a downstream consequence of weight[3].

Why does this matter? Because too many people with "lean PCOS" are told their lab work looks normal, given an oral contraceptive, and sent home — when their insulin is quietly elevated and driving symptoms.

2. The cascade: insulin to androgens to your cycle

Insulin is not just about blood sugar. It is also a powerful signal at the ovary. Here is the simplified, mechanistically supported cascade described in Endocrine Reviews[3]:

  1. Insulin rises — either chronically (insulin resistance with compensatory hyperinsulinemia) or acutely after high-glycemic meals.
  2. The ovaries respond by upregulating androgen production in the theca cells, especially when paired with elevated luteinizing hormone (LH), which is also common in PCOS.
  3. The liver makes less SHBG. Sex hormone-binding globulin is the protein that keeps testosterone bound and biologically inactive. High insulin lowers SHBG production, so a higher fraction of your testosterone becomes "free" — the form that interacts with skin, hair follicles, and the brain.
  4. Free testosterone rises. This is what the body reads. Hence: acne along the jaw, hair thinning at the crown, terminal hair on the chin and jawline, and disrupted ovulatory signaling.
  5. Cycles falter. Follicles arrest mid-development, ovulation becomes inconsistent, and periods become long, light, heavy, or absent.

The point of this picture is empowering, not bleak: if insulin is upstream, then changes that improve insulin signaling tend to improve everything downstream. That is exactly what the trial data show.

3. Symptoms that flag insulin resistance — even at a healthy weight

Insulin resistance is not always loud. But it does leave fingerprints. The National Institute of Diabetes and Digestive and Kidney Diseases (NIDDK) lists several signs you can recognize without a lab[4]:

  • Skin tags and acanthosis nigricans. Velvety, darker patches behind the neck, in the armpits, or under the breasts are classic markers of hyperinsulinemia.
  • Reactive hypoglycemia. A heavy crash 1–3 hours after a carbohydrate-rich meal — shaky, foggy, irritable, hungry again — suggests overshoot insulin response.
  • Strong sugar cravings, especially in the afternoon. Cravings often track with blood-sugar swings rather than willpower.
  • Central (abdominal) weight that is hard to shift, even if your overall BMI is normal.
  • Persistent fatigue, particularly post-meal, alongside difficulty concentrating.
  • Acne on the jaw and lower face, hair thinning at the crown, or new chin or jawline hair — the androgen-driven dermatologic signs of the cascade above.

None of these confirm insulin resistance on their own, but a cluster of them is a strong signal to ask your provider for proper testing.

4. How insulin resistance is actually measured

Here is one of the most important practical points in this article: HbA1c alone is not enough for PCOS.

HbA1c reflects average blood sugar over roughly three months. By the time it is elevated, you are already on the prediabetes-to-diabetes spectrum. Most people with PCOS-related insulin resistance have compensated hyperinsulinemia long before glucose visibly rises. Their bodies are quietly making more insulin to keep glucose in range, and that excess insulin is what is driving the androgen story.

More sensitive options your provider can order:

  • Fasting insulin, ideally drawn alongside fasting glucose. Reference ranges vary, but persistently elevated fasting insulin is meaningful even when glucose is normal.
  • HOMA-IR. A simple calculated index from fasting insulin and fasting glucose: (insulin µIU/mL × glucose mg/dL) ÷ 405. Higher values suggest greater insulin resistance.
  • 2-hour oral glucose tolerance test (OGTT) with insulin curve at 0, 60, and 120 minutes. The 2023 international PCOS guideline recommends an OGTT for risk stratification, especially if fasting markers are borderline or symptoms are prominent[5].
  • HbA1c remains useful as a screening adjunct, but not as a sole test in PCOS.
  • Continuous glucose monitoring (CGM), increasingly available, can show post-meal patterns that single fasting numbers miss.

The American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) practice bulletin recommends screening for impaired glucose tolerance in all women diagnosed with PCOS, with periodic re-screening because risk evolves over time[6].

5. Lifestyle is genuinely first-line — and it works

"Just eat better and exercise" is one of the most condescending pieces of advice anyone gets after a PCOS diagnosis. But the research really does place targeted lifestyle interventions ahead of medication for first-line management. The difference is in the specificity.

A Cochrane systematic review on lifestyle changes for PCOS (Lim and colleagues) concluded that lifestyle interventions improved free androgen index, hirsutism scores, body weight, and fasting insulin compared with usual care[7]. The 2023 international PCOS guideline reinforces that lifestyle remains the cornerstone of long-term management, and that a 5–10% reduction in body weight (where appropriate) is associated with measurable improvements in cycles and metabolic markers[5].

The pieces that consistently move the needle:

  • Protein and fiber at every meal. Both flatten the post-meal glucose curve. The American Heart Association lifestyle scientific statement summarizes the cardiometabolic benefits of fiber-forward, protein-anchored eating patterns[8].
  • Resistance training. Building muscle increases the size of the body's main glucose-disposal tissue. Two to three sessions per week, even short, are enough to start improving insulin sensitivity.
  • Walking after meals. Even 10–15 minutes of post-meal walking reduces glucose excursions in trials of metabolic conditions.
  • Sleep. Short or fragmented sleep impairs insulin sensitivity within days. Aim for 7–9 hours and treat sleep apnea seriously — it is more common in PCOS.
  • Stress. Chronic high cortisol opposes insulin and worsens androgen signaling. The intervention does not have to be elaborate; consistency beats novelty.

6. When medication makes sense

If lifestyle alone is not enough, or if cycles, fertility goals, or metabolic risk demand more support, the evidence base is solid for a few options.

Metformin remains the most-studied insulin-sensitizing medication in PCOS. Both ACOG[6] and the Endocrine Society's PCOS practice guideline support metformin use for metabolic features and, in some cases, menstrual regularity[9]. The 2023 international guideline lists metformin as recommended for adolescents and adults with PCOS in addressing weight, metabolic, and hormonal outcomes alongside lifestyle[5].

Inositol (typically a 40:1 myo-inositol to D-chiro-inositol ratio) is a non-prescription option with growing PCOS-specific evidence. A 2019 meta-analysis in International Journal of Endocrinology by Greff and colleagues found inositol significantly improved insulin sensitivity, total testosterone, and ovulation versus placebo, with comparable effects to metformin in some head-to-head trials[10].

GLP-1 receptor agonists (semaglutide, liraglutide, tirzepatide) are now appearing in PCOS research. Early trials and small meta-analyses suggest improvements in insulin sensitivity, androgens, and weight. The 2023 international PCOS guideline notes that GLP-1 agonists may be considered as adjunct therapy in select cases, but the long-term PCOS-specific evidence is still evolving[5]. This is a "stay-tuned" area, not a gold standard yet.

7. What to ask your doctor — and how to push back gently

If you suspect insulin is part of your PCOS picture, here are concrete asks that can change the appointment:

  • "Can we run fasting insulin alongside fasting glucose, and calculate HOMA-IR?"
  • "Can we do a 2-hour OGTT with insulin levels at 0, 60, and 120 minutes?" (per the 2023 PCOS guideline)[5]
  • "Can we check SHBG, total and free testosterone, and DHEA-S so I can see the androgen side?"
  • "My cycles are X days; what would treatment look like if my goal is regular cycles versus fertility versus symptom control?"
  • "Is metformin or inositol an appropriate option for me at this stage?"

If you are at a healthy weight and have been told to "lose weight" as the answer, a respectful pushback might be: "I understand weight is one factor, but the research shows insulin resistance can be present in lean PCOS too. Can we test it directly?" The Stepto data give that question its scientific footing[2].

8. The mindset shift: insulin resistance is a lever, not a verdict

Here is the part that often gets lost. Insulin resistance is not a moral failing, a punishment, or proof that your body is broken. It is a measurable, modifiable physiological state. Almost everything we have discussed — protein and fiber, sleep, walking, resistance training, inositol, metformin — works because insulin sensitivity is one of the most responsive systems in the body.

Across days and weeks, you can move it. Across months, you can move it a lot. And because insulin is upstream, those improvements ripple outward: clearer skin, calmer cycles, steadier energy, better mood. You will probably not feel a dramatic switch flip; you will notice that things you used to brace for happen less often.

Tracking is what turns this from a vague concept into a feedback loop you can actually steer. When you log cycles, symptoms, glucose readings, supplements, and lifestyle factors over time, you can see which interventions are moving your numbers and which are not. That data is also what makes specialist appointments productive instead of frustrating.

Insulin is not the whole story of PCOS. But it is one of the most leveraged chapters — and it is one you can read, and rewrite, with the right tools.
