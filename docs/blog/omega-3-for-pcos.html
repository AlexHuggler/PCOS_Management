Omega-3 for PCOS: Inflammation, Hormones, and Insulin Sensitivity

If you have PCOS, omega-3 fats are probably the most studied supplement you have not been told enough about. Here is what the evidence actually shows, and how to use it.

By the CycleBalance Team · Reviewed against peer-reviewed research

Key takeaways

  • PCOS involves chronic low-grade inflammation, with elevated CRP roughly 96% higher than in people without PCOS in pooled data.
  • EPA and DHA are the omega-3s that consistently show benefit; ALA from plants converts very inefficiently (often less than 5–10%) to EPA and DHA.
  • Meta-analyses in PCOS suggest omega-3 supplementation can improve insulin resistance markers (HOMA-IR), triglycerides, and total testosterone, with smaller, less consistent effects on inflammation.
  • Practical: aim for two servings of fatty fish per week, then bridge with a third-party tested supplement providing roughly 1–2 g combined EPA + DHA per day.

1. PCOS is also a low-grade inflammatory condition

If you have been told PCOS is "just a hormonal issue," you have only heard half the story. Decades of research now show PCOS sits at the intersection of three connected systems: hormones, metabolism, and inflammation.

A landmark review in Endocrine Reviews by Diamanti-Kandarakis and Dunaif framed PCOS as a metabolic disorder driven in part by insulin resistance, with chronic low-grade inflammation woven through it[1]. A separate review by Repaci and colleagues described how inflammatory cytokines and oxidative stress contribute to ovarian dysfunction in PCOS, helping explain why anti-inflammatory strategies often translate to clinical improvement[2].

The most consistent biomarker is C-reactive protein (CRP). A meta-analysis pooling thousands of participants found that women with PCOS had CRP levels roughly 96% higher than controls, independent of body mass index. That last part matters: even at a "healthy" weight, the inflammatory signal is still elevated. So when we talk about an anti-inflammatory nutrient like omega-3, we are not talking about a fringe wellness trend. We are addressing a measurable feature of the condition.

2. Omega-3 fats 101: ALA, EPA, and DHA

"Omega-3" is a family, not a single nutrient. There are three you should know about:

  • ALA (alpha-linolenic acid). The plant-based omega-3 found in flaxseed, chia, walnuts, and hemp.
  • EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid). A long-chain omega-3 found mainly in fatty fish.
  • DHA (docosahexaenoic acid). Another long-chain omega-3 in fatty fish, important for brain and retinal tissue.

Your body can technically convert ALA to EPA and then to DHA, but the conversion is famously inefficient. The National Institutes of Health Office of Dietary Supplements summarizes the data this way: typically less than 15% of ALA becomes EPA, and even less becomes DHA, with conversion lower in men than premenopausal women[3]. In practical terms, eating chia seeds is good for you, but you should not assume it replaces fatty fish or a fish-oil supplement.

3. What the evidence shows for inflammation in PCOS

If PCOS involves chronic inflammation, can omega-3s actually quiet that signal? The evidence is encouraging but nuanced.

A 2018 systematic review and meta-analysis by Yang and colleagues, published in Reproductive Biology and Endocrinology, pooled randomized controlled trials of omega-3 supplementation in women with PCOS. The authors reported significant improvements in fasting glucose, HOMA-IR, total cholesterol, LDL, and triglycerides versus placebo, alongside more modest reductions in inflammatory markers[4].

An earlier randomized trial by Sadeghi and colleagues, published in European Journal of Clinical Nutrition, tested 2 g/day of omega-3 in women with PCOS over eight weeks and reported improvements in serum lipids and indicators of glycemic control[5]. A 2020 meta-analysis in Nutrition Reviews integrating multiple PCOS trials similarly concluded that omega-3 supplementation lowers triglycerides and improves insulin resistance markers, with smaller, more variable effects on CRP[6].

The takeaway is honest, not hyped: omega-3 is not a magic switch that turns inflammation off, but it is one of the few supplements with consistent metabolic effects in PCOS-specific trials.

4. Omega-3 and insulin sensitivity

Insulin resistance is arguably the central driver of PCOS symptoms. Anything that makes your cells more responsive to insulin tends to translate to better cycles, calmer skin, and steadier energy. So how does omega-3 perform here?

A randomized controlled trial by Mohammadi and colleagues, published in the International Journal of Preventive Medicine, gave women with PCOS 3 g/day of omega-3 for eight weeks. Participants showed reductions in fasting insulin and HOMA-IR compared with placebo[7]. Cussons and colleagues, in The Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism, focused on a related problem: hepatic steatosis (fatty liver) is unusually common in PCOS, and the researchers found that 4 g/day of omega-3 over eight weeks reduced liver fat content and improved triglycerides in women with PCOS[8].

Two important caveats. First, these are short studies (typically 8–12 weeks), so we are observing biomarker shifts rather than long-term outcomes. Second, the absolute changes are meaningful but not enormous. Omega-3 is best understood as one supportive lever, alongside food, movement, and sleep, not a replacement for any of them.

5. Omega-3 and androgens: does it actually lower testosterone?

This is one of the most asked, and most messy, questions about omega-3 in PCOS. The honest answer is: probably yes, modestly, in some people.

Nadjarzadeh and colleagues, in the Iranian Journal of Reproductive Medicine, randomized 78 women with PCOS to 3 g/day of omega-3 or placebo for eight weeks. The omega-3 group showed reductions in serum testosterone and improved menstrual regularity compared with placebo[9]. The Yang 2018 meta-analysis cited above also reported a small but statistically significant reduction in total testosterone with omega-3 versus control across pooled trials[4].

That said, not every trial agrees. Some studies have found no significant change in free or total testosterone, or have shown shifts in sex hormone-binding globulin (SHBG) without clear changes in androgens. The 2023 International Evidence-Based Guideline for the Assessment and Management of PCOS, the most recent global consensus document, places omega-3 in the supportive-evidence category for cardiometabolic profile rather than as a primary androgen-lowering therapy[10]. In plain language: it is reasonable to expect small reductions in testosterone with consistent dosing, but if your acne or hirsutism is severe, omega-3 is a complement, not a substitute, for medical evaluation.

6. Mood, triglycerides, and overall wellbeing

Beyond hormones, omega-3 carries two non-PCOS-specific benefits worth naming because they matter to almost everyone with PCOS.

The first is mood. Anxiety and depression are roughly twice as common in people with PCOS as in the general population. A 2014 meta-analysis of randomized trials in The Journal of Clinical Psychiatry by Grosso and colleagues found that EPA-predominant omega-3 supplementation (typically EPA > 60% of the EPA+DHA total) had a clinically meaningful antidepressant effect in people with diagnosed depression[11]. A more recent meta-analysis by Liao and colleagues in Translational Psychiatry reached a similar conclusion, with the strongest signals in EPA-rich preparations and at doses of 1 g/day or more of EPA[12]. Mood is not a "secondary" issue in PCOS; it is part of the syndrome, and supporting it has knock-on effects on sleep, eating, and motivation.

The second is cardiometabolic risk. The American Heart Association has published a scientific advisory specifically supporting prescription-strength omega-3 (~4 g/day) for high triglycerides, with consistent reductions of 20–30% across trials[13]. Women with PCOS often have elevated triglycerides, so this is one of the few areas where the cardiovascular evidence applies cleanly.

7. Practical: food first, then supplement smartly

A reasonable, evidence-aligned approach looks like this:

  • Food first. Aim for two servings (roughly 250–350 g cooked) of fatty fish per week — salmon, sardines, mackerel, anchovies, herring, or trout. This is the same recommendation echoed across the American Heart Association, NIH ODS, and most national dietary guidelines[3].
  • Use ALA sources as a complement, not a substitute. Ground flaxseed, chia, walnuts, and hemp seeds add fiber and minerals, but conversion to EPA and DHA is too low to count on for hormonal effects.
  • Supplement when food is not enough. Most PCOS trials that showed benefit used 1–4 g/day of combined EPA + DHA. A reasonable starting point is 1–2 g/day of EPA + DHA combined, with dose increases up to 3–4 g/day under medical supervision if you also have elevated triglycerides[13].
  • Read the label carefully. A "1000 mg fish oil" capsule often contains only 300 mg of EPA + DHA. The number that matters is the EPA + DHA content, not the total fish oil weight.
  • Choose third-party tested products. Look for IFOS, USP, or NSF certification to verify potency and purity (heavy metals, oxidation).
  • Vegan or vegetarian? Algae-derived DHA + EPA supplements provide preformed long-chain omega-3s without fish.

8. A brief note on safety

Omega-3 is well tolerated, but it is not zero-risk. At doses above ~3 g/day, fish oil can mildly increase bleeding time, which is mostly relevant if you take anticoagulants (warfarin, DOACs), antiplatelets (aspirin, clopidogrel), or are about to have surgery. The NIH ODS notes that the FDA considers up to 5 g/day of combined EPA + DHA from supplements generally safe for most adults, but always discuss new supplements with your healthcare provider, especially during pregnancy, breastfeeding, or if you take prescription medications[3].

Mild side effects (fishy aftertaste, burping, loose stools) usually fade with food, refrigerated capsules, or split dosing.

Omega-3 will not undo PCOS, but it does sit on the short list of supplements with PCOS-specific evidence behind them, alongside inositol, vitamin D, and magnesium. Consistent food sources, a quality supplement when needed, and patience for 8–12 weeks of use is a reasonable, evidence-aligned plan.
